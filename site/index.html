RECOGNISE

Every wave looks like this. Only the name and the link change.

A real email from this campaign (July 2026), clinic name anonymised. Fake Microsoft share card, "Open Document" button, and the tell-tale "Desktop or Windows Laptop" instruction — because the malware behind it only runs on Windows, hidden by a fake Windows Update screen while a live intruder takes control.

▲"Shared a file / folder with you" from a colleague — especially unexpected
▲Link domain you don't recognise, often ending .vu
▲"Please open on your desktop or Windows laptop" — the single biggest giveaway
▲Link only, no attachment; you're BCC'd ("undisclosed recipients")
▲Arrived at a strange hour — 3–6am is common (overseas operators)
✆The rule that beats every wave: phone the sender before opening any share link. Never verify by replying — the intruder answers their email.

REPORT

Seen one? Report it — the sender is a victim who doesn't know yet.

Every report does two jobs: the clinic whose account was hijacked gets a private phone call so they can lock the intruder out, and the new attack domain goes into the block lists, the browser extension, and takedown reports the same day.

Reports are reviewed by a person. Nothing is published automatically, and affected clinics are never named — the map above shows rounded locations only.

RESPOND

If someone clicked — do this now

  1. Unplug that PC from the network / Wi-Fi immediately and stop using it.
  2. Wipe and reinstall Windows on it. With a live intruder involved, antivirus "cleaning" cannot be trusted.
  3. From a different clean device: change the email password, enable two-factor authentication, sign out all sessions.
  4. Check mail settings for forwarding rules and filters you didn't create; confirm recovery email/phone unchanged; review connected apps.
  5. Assume all browser-saved passwords on that PC are stolen — change them all, banking first (then Medicare/PRODA, HICAPS, practice software, suppliers). Call the bank if card details were saved.
  6. Warn your contacts — your account will be used to attack them next.
  7. Patient data possibly accessed? Call your indemnity provider — the Notifiable Data Breaches scheme (OAIC) may apply.
  8. Report at cyber.gov.au/report and check practice bank accounts and recent invoices for tampering.
HARDEN

Make a click survivable — one afternoon of IT work

  • 2FA on every mailbox, enforced — the single control that stops your practice becoming the next sender.
  • Staff on standard (non-admin) Windows accounts — blocks software installs, which blocks most payloads.
  • Application control: Smart App Control or AppLocker, plus Microsoft Defender ASR rules.
  • DNS filtering at the router (e.g. Quad9 9.9.9.9) — malicious sites fail to load on every device.
  • Mail rule quarantining links to risky endings like .vu — recipes in the IT guide.
  • Tested backups that practice PCs can't overwrite.
  • Referrers & labs: send documents as attachments or via Medical-Objects / HealthLink — never "download from my site" links.
