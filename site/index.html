⚠️ Dental Phish Watch

An active phishing campaign is spreading clinic-to-clinic through Australian dentistry — sent from the real email accounts of hacked colleagues. This page shows you the pattern, collects sightings so affected clinics can be warned, and tells you what to do if you clicked.

TL;DR
  • Email from a real colleague: "[Clinic name] shared a file with you", styled like Microsoft/OneDrive, asking you to open it on a desktop or laptop — that instruction is the giveaway.
  • Link goes to a made-up domain ending in .vu. A new domain is used every wave. Often arrives at 3–6am (overseas attackers).
  • Clicking installs remote-control malware behind a fake "Windows Update" screen. Wipe-and-reinstall territory, not antivirus territory.
  • Verify by phone before opening any share link — even from someone you trust. Never verify by email reply: the hacker answers it.

What the email looks like

A real email from this campaign (July 2026), clinic name anonymised. Every wave looks nearly identical — same fake Microsoft card, same "Open Document" button, same "Desktop or Windows Laptop" line. Only the clinic name and link change.
Red flags — any ONE of these means stop and phone the sender:
  • "Shared a file/folder with you" — especially unexpected
  • Link domain you don't recognise, or ending in .vu
  • "Please open on your desktop / Windows laptop"
  • Link only, no attachment; vague wording
  • BCC'd / "undisclosed recipients"; not addressed by name
  • Arrived at a strange hour (e.g. 3am)

Report a sighting

Received one of these? Reporting it helps two ways: the sender clinic (whose account is hacked and probably doesn't know) gets warned by phone, and new attack domains get added to the block lists and reported for takedown. Reports are reviewed by a person — nothing is published automatically, and the affected clinic is never named publicly.

🔒 Privacy: your report goes only to the site operator for the purpose of warning the affected clinic and reporting the attack infrastructure. Don't paste patient information into this form.

Submitting opens a pre-filled email in your own mail program — you can review exactly what is sent before hitting send. If possible, also attach the original email (in Gmail: ⋮ menu → "Download message").

  • Staff training booklet (Word) — run it as a 15-minute team meeting; add your practice name.
  • Dental Phish Guard browser extension — Chrome & Edge (zip) — flags these emails in Gmail/Outlook web with a red warning banner. If someone clicked — do this now

  1. Unplug that PC from the network / Wi-Fi immediately and stop using it.
  2. Wipe and reinstall Windows on it. With a live attacker involved, antivirus "cleaning" cannot be trusted.
  3. From a different clean device: change the email password, enable two-factor authentication, sign out all sessions.
  4. Check mail settings for forwarding rules and filters you didn't create; confirm recovery email/phone unchanged; review connected apps.
  5. Assume all browser-saved passwords on that PC are stolen — change them all, banking first (then Medicare/PRODA, HICAPS, practice software, suppliers). Call the bank if card details were saved.
  6. Warn your contacts — your account will be used to attack them next.
  7. Patient data possibly accessed? Call your indemnity provider — the Notifiable Data Breaches scheme (OAIC) may apply.
  8. Report at cyber.gov.au/report and check practice bank accounts / recent invoices for tampering.

Protect your practice (one afternoon of IT work)

  • 2FA on every mailbox, enforced — the single control that stops your account becoming the next launchpad.
  • Staff on standard (non-admin) Windows accounts — blocks software installs, which blocks most of these payloads.
  • Application control (Smart App Control / AppLocker) + Microsoft Defender ASR rules.
  • DNS filtering at the router (e.g. Quad9 9.9.9.9); a mail rule quarantining links to risky endings like .vu.
  • Tested backups that practice PCs can't overwrite.
  • Referrers & labs: send documents as attachments or via clinical messaging platforms (Medical-Objects/HealthLink) — never "download from my site" links. Make the scam pattern stand out by convention.
